Carbon Credit and Mangrove Conservation in Malaysia

Mangroves are one of the most important natural ecosystems in Malaysia because they play a major role in protecting the environment and reducing climate change. These coastal forests are part of what scientists call blue carbon ecosystems, together with seagrass meadows and tidal marshes. Unlike land-based or green carbon ecosystems, blue carbon ecosystems store carbon in both the plants and the sediment beneath them.

Mangroves are highly valuable because they absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere and store it for long periods of time. Carbon is stored in their leaves, stems, branches, roots, dead organic matter, and especially in the soil below them. This makes mangroves one of the most effective natural carbon sinks in the world. Although mangroves only cover a very small portion of the Earths coastal areas, they are able to store a disproportionately high amount of organic carbon.

This strong carbon storage ability makes mangroves closely related to the concept of carbon credits. A carbon credit usually represents one metric ton of carbon dioxide that has been removed from the atmosphere or prevented from being released. Because mangroves capture and store large amounts of carbon, they have strong potential to support carbon credit projects and environmental sustainability efforts. In Malaysia, this is especially relevant because the country has extensive mangrove areas that contribute not only to biodiversity and coastal protection, but also to climate mitigation.

In this project, satellite imagery and deep learning are used to support mangrove analysis. Image segmentation techniques, particularly U-Net, help identify mangrove areas from satellite images at pixel level. By detecting the mangrove coverage area more accurately, the system can estimate the size of the plantation area and support further calculation of carbon storage and carbon credit potential.

This approach is important because manual monitoring of mangrove forests can be slow, costly, and difficult to scale. With the help of artificial intelligence, mangrove mapping can become faster, more consistent, and more efficient. As a result, this project can contribute to better environmental monitoring and provide useful insights for sustainable management and carbon-related assessment in Malaysia.

Overall, mangroves are not only important coastal ecosystems, but also valuable natural assets in the fight against climate change. By combining deep learning, satellite imagery, and carbon estimation, this project aims to highlight the environmental and economic value of mangrove conservation in Malaysia.
